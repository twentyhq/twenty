The **data model** is how you describe your business to Twenty. It consists of three ideas: objects, fields and relations. Described well, every fact about your business lives in exactly one place.

Why that matters is easiest to see where it fails. Suppose you make packaging for food brands. A customer rebrands and their logo changes. The change has to reach everything it touches, from open orders to the next production run. A spreadsheet keeps that logo version wherever someone once needed it, across several tabs. Update one tab and the rest are silently wrong. Eventually a print run ships with the old design, at your cost. The problem is structural. A spreadsheet lets the same fact live in many places. Copies drift apart.

## Objects

An **object** is one kind of thing you track, comparable to one tab in a spreadsheet. The packaging business tracks the companies that buy from it, the people at those companies and its open deals. Twenty ships with objects for all of these.

- **Companies**, the organizations you sell to
- **People**, the contacts at those organizations
- **Opportunities**, Twenty's term for deals
- **Tasks** and **Notes**, for the work and context around the rest

Each entry inside an object is a **record**. Harbor Foods is a record in Companies. The purchasing manager there is a record in People.

Most businesses also track something no CRM ships with. The packaging maker cares about print runs. A print run is one batch of boxes going through the presses, with a design, a quantity, a ship date and a destination plant. For that you create a **custom object**. Custom objects behave exactly like built-in ones. They get their own section in the sidebar, their own pages and views, their own API endpoints. In another business the custom object is property listings or job applications.

## Fields

A **field** is one piece of information on a record. In spreadsheet terms, one column. A phone number, an order quantity, a plant address, the current artwork version.

Every field has a **type** that defines what the field holds, whether text, a number, a date, an amount of money or a selection from a list. The type determines what Twenty does with the value.

- **Currency** fields hold amounts Twenty does arithmetic on. A dashboard totals the value of open deals from them.
- **Select** fields hold one value from a fixed list. Deal stages are Select fields. Pipeline columns come straight from them.
- **Date** fields place records on a calendar.
- **Files** fields hold documents, so the artwork PDF lives on the record it describes.

There are more than twenty types in total. Twenty also maintains a few fields automatically on every record, including who created it and when it last changed.

## Relations

Records rarely stand alone. A person works at a company. An order belongs to a customer. A **relation** is a field that holds another record instead of a plain value. On a contact's record, the Company field holds the Harbor Foods record itself rather than the text `Harbor Foods`.

This resolves the rebrand problem. A print run linked to its customer doesn't carry its own copy of the artwork version. The current artwork lives in one place, on the customer's record. Everything linked to that customer reads it from there. The next logo change is one field edit. No order is left pointing at a stale copy.

Relations work in both directions. The Harbor Foods page lists its people, its open deals, its notes and every print run sent to it. Each of those records shows which company it belongs to. This structure is why a rebrand becomes a one-field edit rather than a hunt through tabs.
