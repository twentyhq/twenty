A CRM (Customer Relationship Management system) is a company's shared record of its customers: the people, the conversations, the deals and the commitments. The test of a working one is simple. When a customer writes "as discussed, send over the paperwork", anyone who opens that customer's record knows what was discussed, whether or not they were in the room.

The alternative is what most companies run on by default, customer knowledge spread across inboxes, spreadsheets, chat threads and the memory of whoever handled the last call. That arrangement is free until it isn't. Retrieval fails exactly when it matters. The failure rate compounds with headcount.

## What a CRM records

Each part of that record has a precise shape.

- **People and companies.** Who you do business with, how to reach them and who works where.
- **Conversations.** Every email and meeting, filed against the customer it concerns. Good systems file these automatically.
- **Deals.** The sales in progress. Each carries the buyer, an owner, a value and an expected close date.
- **Tasks and notes.** Commitments made and their deadlines.

## The pipeline

Sales has a natural structure. Every deal sits somewhere between first contact and a decision. A CRM makes that structure explicit. You name the stages and each deal occupies exactly one. A common set is Lead, Demo, Proposal and Won. With the stages as columns, the state of the business becomes a board you can read at a glance. That board is the **pipeline**. Most day-to-day CRM work happens on it.

## When you need one

A five-customer company does not need software for this. A fifty-customer company usually finds out it's overdue. The signs are reliable.

- a paying customer has to re-explain their own history
- two colleagues work the same account without knowing it
- a deal dies of neglect rather than rejection
- nobody can say how many deals are open without calling a meeting

The failure mode of every CRM is staleness. If updating the record takes effort, people skip it and the record quietly stops being true. Twenty's answer is to remove the effort.
