Every company keeps a record of its customers. The only question is where: in one system the whole team maintains, or in fragments spread across inboxes, spreadsheets, chat threads, and the memory of whoever handled the last call. Most companies start with the second option, because at first it costs nothing. The costs come later, and they compound.

A CRM (Customer Relationship Management system) is the first option made deliberate: a single, current account of every customer relationship, shared by the whole team. The test of a working one is simple. When a customer writes "as discussed, send over the paperwork", anyone who opens that customer's record knows what was discussed, whether or not they were in the room.

## What a CRM records

Four things, none of them exotic:

- **People and companies.** The parties you do business with, and how to reach them.
- **Conversations.** Every email and meeting, filed against the customer it concerns.
- **Deals.** The sales in progress. Each carries the buyer, a value, a stage, and an expected close date.
- **Tasks and notes.** Commitments made, and their deadlines.

None of this is new information. What changes is that it lives in one place and stays current, which turns "what's the state of this account?" from an afternoon of asking around into a lookup.

## The pipeline

Sales has a natural structure: every deal sits somewhere between first contact and a decision. A CRM makes that structure explicit. You name the stages, each deal occupies exactly one, and the state of the business becomes a board you can read at a glance: what's closing and what's stuck. That board is the **pipeline**, and most day-to-day CRM work happens on it.

## When you need one

A five-customer company does not need software for this. A fifty-customer company usually discovers it needed it some time ago. The signs are reliable:

- a paying customer has to re-explain their own history
- two colleagues work the same account without knowing it
- a deal dies of neglect rather than rejection
- nobody can say how many deals are open without calling a meeting

The failure mode of every CRM is staleness. If updating the record takes effort, people skip it, and the record quietly stops being true.
