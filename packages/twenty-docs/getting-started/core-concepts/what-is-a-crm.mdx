Early on, a business runs on memory. Whoever spoke to the customer remembers what was agreed, and that's enough.

It stops being enough as the customer count grows. A customer replies "as discussed, send over the paperwork", and the colleague who had that conversation is on leave. The information exists, spread between an inbox and a spreadsheet nobody maintains, but it can't be retrieved at the moment it's needed. Deals stall this way, not because anything went wrong, but because context got lost.

A CRM, short for Customer Relationship Management system, is the fix: one shared, current record of everything your business knows about its customers. Anyone on the team can pick up a customer conversation with the full history in front of them.

## What a CRM keeps track of

A CRM holds four kinds of information, all of which you already have somewhere:

- **People and companies.** Who your customers are and how to reach them.
- **Conversations.** Emails, meetings, and calls, attached to the customer they belong to. A reply that says "as discussed" stops being a research project, because the discussion is on the same page.
- **Deals.** A deal is a potential sale: someone may buy from you but hasn't committed. For each deal you track who it's with, the amount involved, and how far along it is.
- **Tasks and notes.** What was promised, to whom, and by when.

With all of it in one place, questions that used to mean searching an inbox get answered directly: what happened with this account, and which deals are likely to close this month.

## The pipeline

Deals move through steps, from first contact to a final yes or no. In a CRM you give those steps names, and every deal sits in exactly one of them at any time. Shown as columns on a board, this answers "how is business going" with something concrete: how many deals are in each step, and how much they are worth. That view is called the **pipeline**. Much of day-to-day CRM work happens in it, so the word appears throughout these docs.

## Do you need a CRM?

Not immediately. With a handful of customers, a notebook works, and software would add overhead without returning much. There are reliable signals that the time has come:

- you've asked a paying customer to remind you where things stood
- two teammates contacted the same customer in the same week without knowing about each other
- a deal was lost because nobody remembered to follow up

One design consequence is worth knowing up front: a CRM only stays accurate if updating it takes almost no effort. When updating is a chore, people stop doing it, and the data goes stale. This is why Twenty [connects to your inbox and calendar](/getting-started/core-concepts/calendar-and-email) and records the history itself instead of relying on manual entry.
