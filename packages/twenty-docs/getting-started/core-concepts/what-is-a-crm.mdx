You start a business. You make boxes, and companies pack their products in them. The first ten customers are easy to remember: the candle maker, the hot-sauce brand, the bakery down the road. You know their names, their kids' names, which one said "call me again in three months" and meant it. Your memory is the system, and it works.

Then customer number forty replies to an email: "Great. As discussed, send over the paperwork." As discussed *where*? Her name is Sarah, and she runs purchasing at Harbor Foods, the biggest customer you have ever landed. You dig through your inbox. You open a spreadsheet nobody has touched since March. You ask Tom, your teammate who talked to Sarah last week. Tom is on a beach with his phone off until Thursday. Nothing is wrong with your product, your team, or your customer. The sale is dying because everyone forgot.

A CRM exists to stop that. The letters stand for Customer Relationship Management, which is a long name for a simple thing: one place where your business keeps everything it knows about its customers. Think of it as a shared memory. When a teammate picks up a conversation, they can see everything that happened before and continue it, as if they had been in the room all along.

## What a CRM keeps track of

A CRM keeps track of four things, and you already have all of them, just scattered:

- **People and companies.** Who your customers are and how to reach them.
- **Conversations.** The emails, meetings, and calls with each customer, saved on that customer's page. This is how "as discussed" stops being scary: the discussion is right there.
- **Deals.** A deal is a sale you are working on: someone might buy from you, but they haven't said yes yet. For each deal you note who it's with, how much money it could bring, and how far along it is.
- **Tasks and notes.** What you promised to do, for whom, and by when.

With all four in one place, slow questions become fast ones. What happened with this customer? Which deals might finish this month? Who have we not talked to since spring?

## Deals move. The pipeline shows where.

Every deal travels through steps: you meet, they think it over, they say yes or they say no. Give those steps simple names: New, In talks, Won, Lost. Now draw a column for each step, and put every deal in the column where it stands today.

That picture is called a **pipeline**, and it turns the vague question "how's business?" into something you can point at: three deals in talks, two almost won, one lost this week. You'll meet the pipeline everywhere in Twenty, so it's worth knowing the word early.

## Do you need a CRM yet?

With five customers, you honestly don't need a CRM yet. A notebook and a good memory beat any software, and we'd rather not sell you things you don't need. You'll know the day has come when one of these happens:

- you ask a paying customer to remind you what you agreed on
- two teammates contact the same customer in the same week, without knowing about each other
- a sale is lost simply because nobody remembered to follow up

The day one of those happens, it's time.

One more honest thing, because it explains how Twenty is built: a CRM only helps if keeping it up to date is almost no work. Most CRMs die the same way: updating them is a chore, people stop doing it, and soon the tool is full of old, wrong information that nobody trusts. So Twenty [connects to your inbox and calendar](/getting-started/core-concepts/calendar-and-email) and writes the history down for you, instead of hoping you'll do it every evening.
