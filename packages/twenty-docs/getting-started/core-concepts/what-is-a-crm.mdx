Early on, a business runs on memory. Whoever spoke to the customer remembers what was agreed. That's enough.

It stops being enough as the customer count grows. A customer replies "as discussed, send over the paperwork" and the colleague who had that conversation is on leave. The information exists, but it's scattered across inboxes and a spreadsheet nobody keeps up to date. By the time someone finds it, the moment has usually passed.

The fix is a CRM: one shared, current record of everything your business knows about its customers. The name is short for Customer Relationship Management. With one in place, anyone on the team picks up a conversation with the full history in front of them.

## What a CRM keeps track of

A CRM holds four kinds of information, all of which you already have somewhere:

- **People and companies.** Who your customers are and how to reach them.
- **Conversations.** Emails and meetings, attached to the customer they belong to. A reply that says "as discussed" stops being a research project, because the discussion is right there on the customer's page.
- **Deals.** A deal is a potential sale: a buyer who hasn't committed yet. For each deal you track who it's with, the amount, the expected close date, and how far along it is.
- **Tasks and notes.** What was promised, and by when.

With everything in one place, the questions that used to mean digging through an inbox have straight answers: what happened with this account, and which deals are likely to close this month.

## The pipeline

Deals move through steps, from first contact to a final yes or no. In a CRM you give those steps names, and every deal sits in exactly one of them. Lay the steps out as columns on a board and the question "how is business going" gets a concrete answer: how many deals sit in each step, and what they add up to.

That view is called the **pipeline**, and much of day-to-day CRM work happens inside it. The word will keep coming up in these docs.

## When you need one

You don't need a CRM on day one. With a handful of customers, a notebook works fine, and software would mostly get in the way. The signals that it's time:

- you've asked a paying customer to remind you where things stood
- two teammates contacted the same customer in the same week without knowing about each other
- a deal was lost because nobody remembered to follow up
- you can't say how many deals are open right now without asking around

A CRM only stays accurate if updating it takes almost no effort. When updating is a chore, people stop doing it, and the data goes stale.
